# Executive Summary: MTHFR Gene Mutation

**Audience:** Clinicians | **Date:** 2026-05-27 | **Word count:** ~750

---

## Key Findings

The MTHFR gene encodes methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase, an enzyme essential for converting 5,10-methylenetetrahydrofolate to 5-methyltetrahydrofolate — the principal methyl donor for the remethylation of homocysteine to methionine [NLM2019]. Two categories of MTHFR variants have fundamentally different clinical implications and must not be conflated in practice.

**Common polymorphisms (C677T and A1298C):** The 677C>T variant (HGVS: c.665C>T; p.Ala222Val) and 1298A>C variant are the most prevalent MTHFR changes, with combined global prevalence averaging 30–40% [Araszkiewicz2025pmc]. The C677T polymorphism produces a valine-to-alanine substitution at codon 222, reducing enzyme thermostability and decreasing enzyme efficiency by approximately 35% per mutated allele [Araszkiewicz2025pmc; Liew2015]. Homozygosity (TT genotype) produces the greatest enzymatic reduction and most consistently elevated homocysteine. The C677T allele frequency varies substantially by ethnicity: approximately 10–15% in Caucasians, under 2% in African populations, and up to 21% in Hispanic populations [Leclerc2013].

**Rare severe mutations:** At least 109 distinct MTHFR mutations have been catalogued across 171 families, including 70 missense mutations, 17 splice-affecting variants, 11 nonsense mutations, and 7 small deletions [Froese2016]. Only 36% of severe mutations recur in unrelated families, underscoring their heterogeneity. Biallelic pathogenic MTHFR mutations cause severe MTHFR deficiency — the most common genetic cause of elevated plasma homocysteine — presenting clinically with cognitive impairment, feeding difficulties, seizures, and spasticity, characteristically without the haematological abnormalities seen in cobalamin disorders [Huemer2017; Dean2024].

A meta-analysis of 19 case-control studies (2,228 NTD cases, 4,220 controls) confirmed that the fetal MTHFR C677T polymorphism is significantly associated with neural tube defects overall, with a specific association with spina bifida [Tabatabaei2022]. This remains the most robustly supported clinical association of the common polymorphisms.

## Clinical Implications

**Testing:** The ACMG explicitly states that MTHFR polymorphism testing has minimal clinical utility and should not be ordered as part of routine evaluation for thrombophilia or recurrent pregnancy loss [Hickey2013]. The World Thrombosis Day Steering Committee (ISTH) reinforces this position, noting that MTHFR polymorphisms do not meet criteria for inherited thrombophilia and should be excluded from thrombophilia panels [Deloughery2022]. NIH Medical Genetics Summaries similarly advise against genotyping for MTHFR polymorphisms in at-risk family members outside of specific indications [Dean2024]. Testing is appropriate when prior laboratory evaluation has already demonstrated elevated plasma homocysteine, or when severe MTHFR deficiency is clinically suspected [MedlinePlusTest2024].

**Treatment of common polymorphisms:** People with MTHFR polymorphisms can process all forms of folate, including folic acid [CDC2025]. Standard supplementation with 400 mcg/day folic acid prevents neural tube defects regardless of MTHFR genotype [CDC2025]. Supplementation with vitamin B12 and folic acid reduces plasma homocysteine in C677T carriers [Liew2015]. Mandatory grain fortification with folate in the United States since 1996 has rendered the mild hyperhomocysteinemia associated with common MTHFR variants largely clinically irrelevant in the general population [Deloughery2022].

**Treatment of severe MTHFR deficiency:** Betaine (100–150 mg/kg/day, maximum 9 g/day) is the cornerstone of treatment and is strongly recommended; early initiation prevents disease progression and improves outcomes [Huemer2017; Umair2025]. Additional targeted therapies include methionine supplementation, vitamin B12, and L-5-methyltetrahydrofolate (L-5-MTHF) [Umair2025]. Diagnosis requires biallelic pathogenic MTHFR variants plus low plasma methionine, elevated plasma/urine homocysteine, and low cerebrospinal or plasma 5-methyltetrahydrofolate [Umair2025].

---

## Evidence Quality

The evidence base is stratified by variant type. For severe MTHFR deficiency, evidence supporting betaine treatment comes from case series and expert consensus rather than randomized controlled trials, reflecting the rarity of the condition [Huemer2017]. For common polymorphisms and NTD risk, meta-analytic data support a significant association [Tabatabaei2022]. The evidence against MTHFR polymorphism testing for thrombophilia is strong: multiple meta-analyses have disproven an association between hyperhomocysteinemia and coronary heart disease risk, and major professional societies (ACMG, ISTH) have issued formal guideline statements against routine testing [Hickey2013; Deloughery2022]. The CDC and NIH additionally affirm that folate blood levels — not MTHFR genotype — are the relevant determinant of folate sufficiency [CDC2025].

---

## Gaps in Evidence

- The independent clinical contribution of A1298C polymorphism, particularly in compound heterozygosity with C677T, remains insufficiently characterized in large prospective studies.
- There are no randomized controlled trials for betaine treatment in severe MTHFR deficiency; treatment recommendations rest on case series and international expert consensus [Huemer2017].
- The clinical significance of mild hyperhomocysteinemia from common polymorphisms in populations without mandatory food fortification warrants continued investigation.
- Evidence on optimal monitoring intervals and long-term outcomes in treated severe MTHFR deficiency remains limited.
- The appropriate role — if any — of L-5-MTHF versus folic acid supplementation in polymorphism carriers has not been settled by prospective clinical trials.
