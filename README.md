# Research Summary: MTHFR Gene Mutation

## Topic Overview

The methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase (MTHFR) gene encodes an enzyme that plays a central role in folate metabolism. Specifically, MTHFR catalyzes the conversion of 5,10-methylenetetrahydrofolate to 5-methyltetrahydrofolate, the predominant circulating form of folate and the methyl donor required for the remethylation of homocysteine to methionine. Disruption of this enzymatic function — through either rare pathogenic mutations or common polymorphisms — leads to impaired folate cycling and, in significant cases, elevated plasma homocysteine (hyperhomocysteinemia). The two most clinically discussed variants are the common polymorphisms 677C>T (rs1801133) and 1298A>C (rs1801131), which together have a combined global prevalence averaging 30–40% and reduce enzyme efficiency in a dose-dependent manner.

The clinical landscape around MTHFR is divided into two distinct categories that must not be conflated. First, severe MTHFR deficiency caused by rare biallelic pathogenic mutations results in a serious metabolic disorder (homocystinuria) presenting with cognitive impairment, seizures, spasticity, and markedly elevated homocysteine, requiring active medical management. Second, the common MTHFR polymorphisms (C677T and A1298C) produce mild to moderate reductions in enzyme activity that, while biochemically measurable, have uncertain clinical significance in the era of mandatory folic acid food fortification. Major professional societies — including the American College of Medical Genetics (ACMG), the International Society on Thrombosis and Haemostasis (ISTH), and NIH-affiliated institutions — explicitly advise against routine MTHFR polymorphism testing for thrombophilia, recurrent pregnancy loss, or population screening, citing lack of clinical utility.

This document synthesizes peer-reviewed literature, NIH reference materials, and current clinical guidelines to provide a rigorous, evidence-based overview of MTHFR gene variants for use by clinicians, researchers, and informed patients. Readers are encouraged to distinguish between the well-established clinical entity of severe MTHFR deficiency and the far more common, but clinically ambiguous, MTHFR polymorphisms, which have been subject to significant overmedicalization in direct-to-consumer genetic testing markets.
