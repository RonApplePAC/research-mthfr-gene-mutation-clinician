# Clinical Deep Dive: MTHFR Gene Mutation

**Audience:** Clinicians | **Date:** 2026-05-27

---

## 1. Background and Pathophysiology

The methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase (MTHFR) enzyme occupies a pivotal position in one-carbon metabolism, operating at the intersection of folate and methionine cycles. The MTHFR gene, located on chromosome 1p36.3, encodes this flavoprotein that irreversibly reduces 5,10-methylenetetrahydrofolate to 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF) — the predominant circulating form of folate in humans [NLM2019]. This reaction is the sole physiological route for generating the methyl group that remethylates homocysteine to methionine, a reaction also requiring cobalamin (vitamin B12) as a cofactor and catalyzed by methionine synthase [Araszkiewicz2025].

When MTHFR activity is reduced or absent, the downstream consequences include: (1) reduced availability of 5-MTHF for homocysteine remethylation; (2) accumulation of plasma homocysteine (hyperhomocysteinemia); and (3) reduced methionine synthesis, which in severe cases impairs the methionine cycle and downstream methylation reactions including DNA methylation and neurotransmitter synthesis [Araszkiewicz2025; Dean2024].

The clinical spectrum of MTHFR-related disease spans from the common, largely benign polymorphisms affecting tens of millions of people worldwide, to severe biallelic mutations causing a rare but serious inborn error of metabolism. Understanding this distinction is essential for appropriate clinical decision-making.

## 2. Mechanism of Action

### Enzyme Biochemistry

MTHFR functions as a homodimer and uses flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) as a cofactor. The enzyme's activity is regulated allosterically by S-adenosylmethionine (SAM), which inhibits MTHFR when methionine and SAM levels are adequate — providing feedback control of the methionine cycle [Leclerc2013].

### Common Polymorphisms: Molecular Effects

**677C>T (rs1801133; HGVS: c.665C>T; p.Ala222Val):** This is the most extensively studied common MTHFR variant. The single nucleotide change produces a valine-to-alanine substitution at codon 222 of the mature protein [UFPathLabs2024; Liew2015]. This substitution reduces the enzyme's affinity for its FAD cofactor, resulting in increased thermolability of the enzyme — meaning activity is disproportionately lost at physiologic or febrile temperatures. Enzyme efficiency is reduced by approximately 35% per mutated allele, with heterozygotes (CT genotype) showing intermediate reduction and homozygotes (TT genotype) showing the most pronounced activity loss [Araszkiewicz2025pmc]. The TT genotype consistently produces higher fasting plasma homocysteine compared to CT and CC genotypes, though the magnitude of elevation depends heavily on folate status.

**1298A>C (rs1801131):** This variant results in a glutamate-to-alanine substitution. It reduces enzyme activity but does not increase thermolability. The 1298A>C variant has a smaller effect on homocysteine when present alone, but compound heterozygosity (677CT + 1298AC) produces an enzyme activity profile approaching that of C677T homozygosity [Araszkiewicz2025].

### Population Genetics

The 677C>T allele demonstrates striking ethnic variation in prevalence. Allele frequencies are approximately 10–15% in Caucasian populations, under 2% in African populations, and up to 21% in Hispanic populations [Leclerc2013]. Globally, combined prevalence of the two major polymorphisms averages 30–40% [Araszkiewicz2025pmc]. Up to 50% of people carry at least one change in an MTHFR gene [ClevelandClinic2024].

## 3. Clinical Evidence

### Neural Tube Defects

The most robustly demonstrated clinical association of the common MTHFR polymorphisms is with neural tube defects (NTDs). A systematic review and meta-analysis by Tabatabaei et al. (2022) pooled data from 19 case-control studies comprising 2,228 NTD cases and 4,220 controls [Tabatabaei2022]. The analysis found a statistically significant correlation between fetal MTHFR C677T polymorphism and NTD development in the overall population, with a specific and significant association identified for spina bifida. This association is biologically plausible given the role of folate in neural tube closure during embryogenesis. Notably, adequate periconceptional folic acid supplementation substantially reduces NTD risk regardless of maternal or fetal MTHFR genotype [CDC2025].

### Hyperhomocysteinemia

The C677T variant is recognized as the most common genetic cause of mild to moderate hyperhomocysteinemia [Leclerc2013; Dean2024]. The degree of homocysteine elevation correlates with genotype (TT > CT > CC) and is modifiable by folate and B12 status. Liew and Das Gupta (2015) confirmed that supplementation with vitamin B12 and folic acid reduces plasma homocysteine in C677T carriers [Liew2015], providing a practical intervention point.

### Thrombophilia — Lack of Evidence

Despite widespread inclusion of MTHFR polymorphism testing in commercial thrombophilia panels, the evidence does not support this practice. Meta-analyses have disproven an independent association between hyperhomocysteinemia attributable to common MTHFR variants and coronary heart disease [Hickey2013]. The World Thrombosis Day Steering Committee (ISTH) published a formal call to action in 2022 noting that MTHFR polymorphisms do not meet the criteria for inherited thrombophilia and should be excluded from thrombophilia workup panels [Deloughery2022]. This position is reinforced by the observation that mandatory folic acid fortification of grain products in the United States since 1996 has rendered the mild hyperhomocysteinemia associated with common MTHFR variants largely irrelevant to cardiovascular risk stratification [Deloughery2022].

### Severe MTHFR Deficiency

The rare, severe form of MTHFR deficiency is caused by biallelic pathogenic mutations. Froese et al. (2016) catalogued 109 distinct MTHFR mutations across 171 families, including 70 missense mutations, 17 splice-affecting variants, 11 nonsense mutations, and 7 small deletions [Froese2016]. Only 36% of these severe mutations recur in unrelated families, underscoring the genetic heterogeneity of this condition. Severe MTHFR deficiency is the most common genetic cause of elevated plasma homocysteine [Dean2024] and typically presents in infancy or childhood, though late-onset cases are recognized. The clinical phenotype includes cognitive impairment, feeding difficulties, seizures, spasticity, and gait abnormalities — but, importantly, without the haematological abnormalities (megaloblastic anemia) typically seen in cobalamin disorders [Huemer2017].

## 4. Current Guidelines

**ACMG (2013):** The American College of Medical Genetics issued a practice guideline stating that MTHFR polymorphism testing has minimal clinical utility and should not be ordered as part of routine evaluation for thrombophilia or recurrent pregnancy loss, and should not be used to screen at-risk family members [Hickey2013].

**ISTH/World Thrombosis Day Steering Committee (2022):** A formal consensus statement concluded that MTHFR polymorphisms are not inherited thrombophilias, should be removed from thrombophilia testing panels, and that clinicians should counsel patients who have had this testing that results do not predict thrombosis risk [Deloughery2022].

**NIH Medical Genetics Summaries (2024):** Affirms that MTHFR polymorphism genotyping should not be ordered in clinical evaluation for thrombophilia, recurrent pregnancy loss, or for at-risk family members [Dean2024].

**CDC (2025):** States that all people — regardless of MTHFR genotype — can process folic acid, and that 400 mcg/day of folic acid prevents neural tube defects [CDC2025]. Folic acid blood levels, not MTHFR genotype, are the relevant clinical determinant.

**JIMD Guidelines for Severe MTHFR Deficiency (2017):** Huemer et al. strongly recommend betaine treatment in all individuals with confirmed severe MTHFR deficiency, noting that early initiation prevents disease and improves outcomes [Huemer2017]. The guideline provides specific guidance on diagnosis, treatment initiation, and monitoring in the context of remethylation disorders.

## 5. Dosing and Administration

### Common Polymorphisms

No specific pharmacological treatment is indicated for common MTHFR polymorphisms in otherwise healthy individuals. Standard supplementation:
- **Folic acid:** 400 mcg/day for general population; 400–800 mcg/day periconceptionally for women planning pregnancy [CDC2025]
- **Vitamin B12:** Standard dietary intake or supplementation to maintain normal serum B12 is appropriate for reducing homocysteine in carriers [Liew2015]
- There is no established evidence from clinical trials supporting the use of L-5-MTHF (methylfolate) over standard folic acid for common polymorphism carriers [CDC2025]

### Severe MTHFR Deficiency

Per GeneReviews (2025) and JIMD guidelines (2017):
- **Betaine:** 100–150 mg/kg/day (maximum 9 g/day); primary remethylation therapy bypassing the MTHFR step [Umair2025; Huemer2017]
- **Methionine supplementation:** Used to address methionine deficiency
- **Vitamin B12 (hydroxocobalamin or cyanocobalamin):** Cofactor support for methionine synthase
- **L-5-MTHF:** Exogenous 5-methyltetrahydrofolate supplementation [Umair2025]
- Folate-restricted diet is generally not indicated; protein restriction may be used adjunctively in some protocols per specialist guidance

## 6. Contraindications and Adverse Effects

### Common Polymorphisms

Standard folate and B12 supplementation at recommended doses carries minimal adverse effect risk. High-dose folate supplementation may mask vitamin B12 deficiency, particularly in older adults — clinicians should assess B12 status before prescribing high-dose folate.

### Severe MTHFR Deficiency — Betaine

High-dose betaine has been associated with cerebral edema in a subset of patients with concurrent hypermethioninemia, particularly in homocystinuria subtypes. Methionine levels should be monitored during betaine therapy to avoid excessive elevation [Huemer2017]. Betaine is generally well tolerated at recommended doses; fishy body odor and gastrointestinal discomfort are occasionally reported.

## 7. Special Populations

### Pregnancy

Women with the MTHFR C677T TT genotype may have modestly increased risk of NTDs in offspring; however, current evidence supports standard periconceptional folic acid (400–800 mcg/day) as the intervention of choice regardless of genotype [CDC2025; Tabatabaei2022]. There is no evidence that higher-dose folate or L-5-MTHF is superior to folic acid in this context for women with common polymorphisms. MTHFR polymorphism testing is not recommended as part of recurrent pregnancy loss evaluation [Dean2024; Hickey2013].

### Pediatric Patients (Severe Deficiency)

Severe MTHFR deficiency often presents in infancy or early childhood. Newborn screening does not routinely identify MTHFR deficiency; diagnosis is typically prompted by clinical features (hypotonia, developmental delay, seizures) combined with biochemical findings. Early diagnosis and treatment with betaine is critical — outcomes are substantially better with early intervention [Huemer2017]. A multidisciplinary team including metabolic specialists, neurology, and dietetics is recommended.

### Elderly

Hyperhomocysteinemia from any cause — including MTHFR polymorphisms — may be more clinically relevant in elderly populations with poor nutritional status and B12 deficiency. Assessment of folate and B12 status is appropriate; treatment targets the nutritional deficiency rather than the genotype.

### Populations Without Food Fortification

The clinical significance of MTHFR C677T homozygosity is greater in populations not benefiting from mandatory folic acid fortification. In these settings, the mild homocysteine elevation may warrant dietary counseling and folate supplementation [Deloughery2022; CDC2025].

## 8. Monitoring Parameters

### Common Polymorphisms

- **Plasma homocysteine:** Fasting plasma homocysteine is the relevant functional biomarker; genotype alone does not require monitoring. Testing is indicated when high homocysteine is found on initial workup.
- **Serum folate and B12:** Assess nutritional status and guide supplementation, not genotype-driven intervention.
- MTHFR genetic test results should be communicated and contextualized by trained genetics professionals [UFPathLabs2024].

### Severe MTHFR Deficiency

Recommended monitoring per Huemer et al. (2017) and GeneReviews (2025):
- **Plasma total homocysteine:** Serial measurement to assess treatment response; targets are treatment-center specific but generally aim for reduction toward the normal range
- **Plasma methionine:** Monitor for excess elevation during betaine therapy
- **Plasma/CSF 5-methyltetrahydrofolate:** Assess adequacy of folate supplementation
- **Neurodevelopmental assessment:** Serial developmental evaluation in pediatric patients
- **MRI brain:** Baseline and follow-up to monitor for white matter disease progression
