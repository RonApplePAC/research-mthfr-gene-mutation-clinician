# Understanding MTHFR Gene Variants: A Patient Guide

**Prepared for patients by your care team | 2026-05-27**

---

## What Is It?

MTHFR stands for methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase. It is a gene in your body that gives instructions for making an important enzyme — a protein that helps your body process folate (a B vitamin).

Most people have heard about MTHFR because of genetic tests sold directly to consumers. The truth is that MTHFR gene changes — called variants or polymorphisms — are very common. Up to half of all people have at least one change in this gene. Having a common MTHFR variant does not mean you have a disease.

There is a separate, rare condition called severe MTHFR deficiency. It is caused by two faulty copies of the gene (one from each parent). This is much less common and has very different health effects. Your doctor can tell you which situation applies to you.

---

## How Does It Work?

Your body uses folate from food and vitamins to carry out important jobs — like making new cells and keeping a substance called homocysteine at safe levels. The MTHFR enzyme helps convert folate into a form your body can actually use for these jobs.

When a common MTHFR gene change is present, the enzyme works a little less efficiently. For most people, this makes a very small difference because eating enough folate — through food or a daily vitamin — easily makes up for it. Your body can handle all types of folate just fine, even with a common MTHFR variant.

In the rare severe form of MTHFR deficiency, the enzyme barely works at all. This causes serious problems starting in childhood, including developmental delays and seizures. Doctors treat this with specific medicines and supplements.

---

## What Does the Research Show?

Here are the key things researchers have found:

- **Common MTHFR variants are widespread.** Up to half of all people have one. Because they are so common, they are considered a normal variation in the human population — not a disease.

- **Standard folic acid works for everyone.** People with common MTHFR variants can process folic acid normally. Taking 400 micrograms (mcg) of folic acid every day — especially before and during early pregnancy — helps prevent serious birth defects called neural tube defects. This recommendation does not change based on your MTHFR results.

- **Testing is usually not needed.** Major medical organizations, including the American College of Medical Genetics, say that MTHFR gene testing has very little value for most people. Knowing your MTHFR result usually does not change what your doctor will recommend. The test is mainly useful if a blood test has already shown that your homocysteine level is high.

- **High homocysteine is the real target.** If a blood test shows your homocysteine is elevated, your doctor may look for causes — including nutritional deficiencies in folate and B12. Treatment targets those levels, not the gene variant itself.

- **The rare severe form needs specialized care.** If you or your child has been diagnosed with severe MTHFR deficiency, a specialist will guide treatment. A medication called betaine is the main treatment and works best when started early.

---

## What Should I Ask My Doctor?

If you have had MTHFR testing or are thinking about it, here are some helpful questions:

- "Do I actually need this test, or would a homocysteine blood test give more useful information?"
- "If my result comes back positive, will it change my treatment plan?"
- "Should I be taking a daily folic acid or B vitamin supplement, and at what dose?"
- "If I am pregnant or planning to become pregnant, what is the right folic acid dose for me?"
- "Is there a genetic counselor I can speak with about my results?"

Remember: a common MTHFR variant is not a diagnosis. It is one small piece of your genetic makeup. For most people, eating a healthy diet, taking a daily multivitamin or folic acid supplement, and seeing your doctor regularly are the most important steps — regardless of MTHFR status.
